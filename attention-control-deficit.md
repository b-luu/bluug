## Full post (blog / Medium)

**The attention control deficit nobody talks about**
*Attention isn't binary, it's a dial.*

---

I'm discovering a life without attention black holes. And it's liberating.

Everyone talks about attention deficit. Not enough of it, can't hold it, phone keeps stealing it. That's not my problem.

My problem is the opposite. I've always had too much attention, but with no say over where it goes. When something catches, everything else drops. Other work, sleep, conversations, whatever. Gone. I don't resurface until either the thing is done, or I am.

That's the attention control deficit nobody talks about: not the inability to focus, but the inability to stop.

I've learned to work around it. I stay away from social media mostly, because I know that if I fall in, that becomes the one thing, and there's no algorithmic feed worth sacrificing sleep over. Occasionally I do let my attention wander off in wonder, but on purpose. That's actually where the interesting stuff happens. The unexpected connection, the idea you didn't go looking for. Exploration, not exploitation. But it only works in small doses, and with an exit.

What's changed recently is AI.

Not in the way people usually mean it. AI didn't fix my focus or make me more productive in the obvious sense. What it did is remove the traps. The things I used to fall into for hours: the gnarly implementation detail, the rabbit hole of getting tooling to cooperate, those just... resolve now. Fast. AI handles the technicalities that used to swallow me whole.

Which means my attention is actually free for the first time. Free to move, to explore, to come back. No more black holes.

I'm still figuring out what that actually means for me. But in the middle of last night, attention spinning in the dark as usual, I landed on this: attention isn't binary. It's a dial. You can learn to turn it up when you need depth, down when you need breadth, and, this is the part that's new for me: actually get it back when you need to move on!

Used to be easier said than done. Maybe now it isn't, thanks to AI?

---

*PS: the 3AM inspiration spike is real and recurring, and rarely enjoyable: tomorrow-me always pays the price. If you've found a way to quiet your brain for the entire night, every night, and without the grogginess the next day, I'm genuinely all ears.*
